Consider the behavior of two machines in a distributed system. Both have clocks that are supposed to tick 1000 times per millisecond. One of them actually does, but the other ticks only 990 times per millisecond. If UTC updates come in once a minute, what is the maximum clock skew that will occur?

The clock skew is 1000-990 times per millisecond=10 times per millisecond=10000 times per second=60000 times per minute.

To achieve totally-ordered multicasting with Lamport timestamps, is it strictly necessary that each message is acknowledged?

No, it is sufficient to follow Lamport's algorithm, that is, the sent message has a timestamp larger than the received message.

Suppose that two processes detect the demise of the coordinator simultaneously and both decide to hold an election using the bully algorithm. What happens?

The election is going to proceed as expected because the second election message is ignored even though the bullies will get two election messages. When an election starts with a bully algorithm, it must finish and ignore intermediate elections.

In Fig. 6-21 we have two ELECTION messages circulating simultaneously. While it does no harm to have two of them, it would be more elegant if one could be killed off. Devise an algorithm for doing this without affecting the operation of the basic election algorithm.

On the election message reception, the process has two alternatives, if it is its own election, it turns the message as usual. Otherwise, it has other two alternatives, if it is a competitor with a lower number and sends a message before, the process discards the election. If the competitor has a higher number, the message is forwarded as usual.
